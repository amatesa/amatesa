# Chapter 05 — Looking Forward

I've never been very good at planning my career around job titles.

Looking back, my path doesn't follow a straight line.

Healthcare.

Operations.

Customer support.

Data analysis.

Web development.

Artificial intelligence.

Game development.

At first glance, they seem unrelated.

Today I see something different.

Each step gave me another way of understanding how people work, how systems evolve, and how problems can be solved.

None of those experiences replaced the previous one.

They accumulated.

Every new discipline became another perspective rather than another identity.

That's probably why I don't define myself by a single profession.

The problems that interest me rarely fit inside one discipline.

Digital products combine technology, design, business, communication, psychology and people.

Games combine storytelling, interaction, systems and emotion.

Artificial intelligence combines learning, experimentation and decision-making.

Operations combine processes, documentation and collaboration.

They're different fields.

But they all reward the same habit.

Understanding before building.

Looking ahead, I don't have a fixed destination.

I have a direction.

I want to keep working on products that improve the way people learn, work, create or interact.

I want to keep joining projects that make me curious.

Projects that force me to learn something new.

Projects where asking good questions is as valuable as writing good code.

I know there will always be people who know more than I do about a specific technology.

That has never bothered me.

What I hope to bring is something different.

The ability to connect ideas.

To understand unfamiliar contexts.

To keep learning without being afraid of starting from zero.

To improve systems instead of simply maintaining them.

To leave things better than I found them.

That is the professional I continue trying to become.

Not because I believe there is a final version of myself waiting somewhere in the future.

But because improvement has never been a destination.

It has always been the way I move forward.

And if one day someone looks back at my work, I hope they don't remember the number of technologies I learned or the titles I had.

I hope they remember something much simpler.

That I cared.

That I did the work well.

And that the things I touched worked a little better afterwards.
