# Chapter 01 — Understanding

Most people start with solutions.

I usually start somewhere else.

I try to understand.

When I enter a new project, a new company, or even a completely unfamiliar field, my first instinct isn't to change things. It's to observe. I read the documentation, compare it with what actually happens, ask questions, and try to understand why people work the way they do.

Over the years I've learned that the problem people describe is often only the visible symptom. The real challenge usually sits somewhere deeper: in a process, in missing information, in unclear communication, or in assumptions that no one has questioned for a long time.

That's the part I enjoy.

I like finding those hidden connections.

It doesn't matter whether I'm analyzing operational data, investigating a technical issue, designing part of a game, building a website, or documenting a workflow. The industries have changed throughout my career, but the process has remained remarkably consistent.

Understand first.

Build later.

That approach has taken me through healthcare, fintech, customer operations, web development, data analysis, artificial intelligence, and game development. Every new field forced me to learn different tools, but none of those tools became my identity.

The process did.

I don't believe expertise begins with memorizing technology.

I believe it begins with understanding the problem well enough to know which knowledge is actually required.

Sometimes that means learning a new framework.

Sometimes it means writing documentation.

Sometimes it means asking better questions.

Sometimes it means realizing that the original problem wasn't the real problem at all.

I've never been interested in collecting technologies simply to list them on a résumé.

What motivates me is something much simpler.

Finding a better way.

A simpler process.

A clearer explanation.

A more useful product.

A better question.

Small improvements accumulate.

Over time they become meaningful change.

Looking back, I realize that every role I've had has taught me the same lesson in a different context.

The environment changes.

The tools change.

The people change.

But understanding always comes first.

Everything else comes after.
